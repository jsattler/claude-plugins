description: Set up your cycling coach profile and goals
---

Welcome the athlete and gather the information needed to coach them effectively. This is a first-time setup conversation.

## Step 1: Pull existing data

Retrieve the athlete's profile and recent fitness summary from Intervals.icu to understand their current state. Summarize what you find: current FTP, CTL, recent training volume, configured zones.

## Step 2: Analyze training schedule patterns

Before asking the athlete about their availability, look at their actual behavior. Pull activities from the last 4-6 weeks from Intervals.icu and analyze:

- **Which days of the week** they typically ride (e.g., "You've ridden on Tuesdays, Thursdays, and Saturdays most weeks")
- **What time of day** rides happen (use activity start times — morning, midday, evening)
- **How long** sessions are on each day (e.g., "Weekday rides average 70 minutes, weekend rides average 2.5 hours")
- **Consistency patterns** — are some days almost always used for riding while others are sporadic?

Present these findings to the athlete as a starting point: "Looking at your past month, it seems like you typically ride on [days], with weekday rides around [duration] and longer rides on [day]. Does that match your actual availability, or has your schedule changed?"

## Step 3: Ask about goals and schedule

Use AskUserQuestion to learn:

- What is your cycling focus? (e.g., endurance/gran fondo, road racing/crits, time trialing, general fitness)
- What is your primary goal event or target? (e.g., a specific gran fondo, general endurance fitness, hitting a CTL target)
- When is your goal event, if applicable?
- Do you have any physical limitations, injuries, or constraints to be aware of?

## Step 4: Confirm weekly availability

Based on the patterns from Step 2, confirm and refine the athlete's real-world schedule. Use AskUserQuestion to ask:

- **Which days can you reliably train?** (Pre-fill with the pattern you observed, let them correct)
- **How much time do you have on each training day?** This is the key question — understand their real constraints. Examples:
  - "I work 9-5, so weekdays I can only ride before 7:30am or after 6pm — that gives me about 60-90 minutes"
  - "Wednesdays I work from home so I can squeeze in a longer lunch ride"
  - "Saturday mornings are free but I need to be done by noon for family time"
  - "Sundays are flexible — I can go as long as I want"
- **Do you have other regular training?** (running, strength, swimming — what days and roughly how long)
- **What typically disrupts your training?** (travel, kids' schedules, seasonal daylight changes, weather preferences for indoor vs outdoor)

Build a clear picture of the athlete's weekly time budget, not just total hours, but which hours on which days.

## Step 5: Assess current fitness

Based on the Intervals.icu data and the athlete's answers:

- Summarize their current fitness level in plain language
- Identify strengths (e.g., strong aerobic base, good climbing power) and limiters (e.g., low time at threshold, inconsistent volume)
- Compare their available training time to what their goals require
- Note if their schedule constrains certain workout types (e.g., only 60-min weekday slots means threshold intervals need to be compact)

## Step 6: Outline a coaching approach

Provide a brief overview of how you'll approach their training:

- Which periodization phase they should be in given their goal timeline
- What the weekly training structure might look like, mapped to their actual schedule (e.g., "Tuesday morning: 60-min sweetspot before work. Thursday evening: 75-min tempo. Saturday: long endurance ride, 2.5-3.5 hours")
- Key areas to focus on first
- Brief nutrition considerations based on their training volume and goals — point them to `/nutrition` for a deeper dive

Save a summary of this conversation by telling the athlete what you've learned and what the plan is. Encourage them to use `/weekly-review` each week, `/plan-week` to get their upcoming training, and `/nutrition` for fueling guidance.
